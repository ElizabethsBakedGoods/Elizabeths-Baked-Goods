My Top 5 Mistakes Beginners Make When Baking Macarons

By Elizabeth – Published December 2025

Macarons are one of the most elegant and delicate desserts you can make at home. Their crisp shells, soft centers, and endless flavor possibilities make them a favorite for special occasions, holidays, and gifts. But if you've ever tried to bake macarons, you know that they can be intimidating — even for seasoned bakers. I've spent years perfecting my technique, and along the way, I've seen beginners make the same mistakes repeatedly. In this blog, I'll break down the top five mistakes beginners make when baking macarons and how to avoid them so you can create bakery-quality macarons at home.

1. Not Measuring Ingredients Accurately

Macarons are a precise dessert. Unlike cookies or brownies, even a slight imbalance in ingredients can result in cracked shells, hollow centers, or uneven texture. Beginners often rely on cups or spoons, but this is not accurate enough for macarons. Always use a kitchen scale to measure your almond flour, powdered sugar, and egg whites. Weighing ingredients ensures consistency every time.

Tip: After sifting your almond flour and powdered sugar, weigh the mixture again to make sure you haven't lost any volume. Even small changes can affect the macaronage — the crucial mixing stage where your batter reaches the perfect consistency.

2. Overmixing or Undermixing the Batter

The macaron batter, or macaronage, is one of the trickiest parts of the process. Beginners often make one of two mistakes: undermixing or overmixing. Undermixed batter will be too stiff, resulting in macarons that are lumpy and crack during baking. Overmixed batter, on the other hand, will be too runny and spread too much, losing that signature round shape.

Here's a simple test: when you lift your spatula and let the batter fall back into the bowl, it should flow slowly and form a thick ribbon. If it sits on top of the rest of the batter without flowing, it's too stiff. If it sinks too quickly, it's too runny. Practice and observation are key here — patience makes perfect.

3. Skipping the Resting Period Before Baking

One of the most common mistakes is skipping the resting period before baking. Once you pipe your macarons onto the baking sheet, you need to let them sit at room temperature for 20–60 minutes, depending on humidity. This allows a skin to form on the surface, which helps create the classic smooth shell and prevents cracking.

Tip: Lightly touch the top of a macaron with your finger — it should feel dry to the touch. If it's still sticky, let it rest longer. Skipping this step is one of the fastest ways to end up with cracked shells or uneven feet.

4. Baking at the Wrong Temperature

Oven temperature is another common stumbling block. Even a difference of 10–15°F can affect your macarons. Beginners often bake at too high a temperature, causing browning, hollow shells, or uneven feet. Others bake too low, leaving the macarons undercooked or flat.

Tip: Use an oven thermometer to check your actual temperature. Start at 300°F (150°C) for most macarons and adjust slightly based on your oven. Each oven is different, so test batches are essential. Also, rotate your baking sheets halfway through baking for even results.

5. Choosing the Wrong Ingredients or Substitutions

Beginners often try to substitute ingredients in macaron recipes — and this can lead to disaster. Macarons rely on the delicate balance of almond flour, powdered sugar, and egg whites. Using almond meal instead of finely ground almond flour, or skipping the powdered sugar, can drastically alter the texture and appearance.

Tip: Use fresh, high-quality ingredients. Egg whites should be aged at room temperature for at least 24 hours to reduce moisture content, which helps create stronger shells. Finely sift your almond flour and powdered sugar together for a smooth, even batter.

Bonus Tips for Beginner Macaron Bakers

  • Practice makes perfect. Don't be discouraged by the first few batches — even experienced bakers have trial-and-error days.
  • Use silicone baking mats or parchment paper for even heat distribution.
  • Experiment with flavors and colors once you've mastered the basic technique — peppermint, chocolate, or fruity fillings are fun ways to personalize your macarons.
  • Consider keeping a macaron baking journal to note what worked and what didn't each time you bake.

Final Thoughts

Baking macarons is a rewarding challenge. While beginners often make these five mistakes, knowing what to look for and taking your time can help you produce perfect, bakery-quality macarons at home. Remember: precise measurements, correct mixing, proper resting, accurate baking temperature, and high-quality ingredients are the keys to success.

If you'd like, you can also order a batch of artisan macarons from my bakery to see how they should look and taste. Studying professional results can help guide your own practice and give you inspiration for flavors, colors, and presentation.

With patience, practice, and attention to detail, your macarons will be the star of any table — from holiday gatherings to special gifts. Happy baking!
